BAUHAUS
DESIGN
MOVEMENT

The Bauhaus was a German art school operational from 1919 to 1933 that combined crafts and the fine arts. It played a pivotal role in the development of modern architecture and design, emphasizing functionality, simplicity, and the integration of art into everyday life.

PRINCIPLES

Form follows function. The school promoted the idea that design should serve practical purposes while maintaining aesthetic appeal. It rejected ornamentation in favor of clean, geometric forms.

INFLUENCE

Bauhaus influenced modern design, architecture, and typography. Its legacy includes the use of primary colors, sans-serif fonts, and modular construction in furniture and buildings.

Functionality
Geometry
Innovation

Historical Context

Founded in 1919 in Weimar, Germany, by architect Walter Gropius, the Bauhaus aimed to bridge the gap between art and industry. It relocated to Dessau in 1925 and then to Berlin in 1932 before being closed by the Nazis in 1933 due to its progressive ideals.

Key Principles

Unity of Art and Technology

The Bauhaus believed that artists and craftsmen should collaborate to create functional, beautiful objects for mass production.

Emphasis on Experimentation

Students were encouraged to explore materials, forms, and techniques through workshops in metalworking, weaving, pottery, and more.

Social Responsibility

Design should improve quality of life, making good design accessible to everyone, not just the elite.

Influential Figures

  • Walter Gropius: Founder and director, emphasized interdisciplinary education.
  • Paul Klee and Wassily Kandinsky: Taught foundational courses in color theory and form.
  • Mies van der Rohe: Last director, known for "less is more" philosophy.
  • László Moholy-Nagy: Pioneered photography and kinetic art.
